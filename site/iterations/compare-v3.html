storage for video teams, compared · june 2026

Same workflow. Different bill.

Suite, Shade, and LucidLink sell the mounted-drive workflow by the seat and the terabyte, forever. JuiceMount is the open-source alternative — the same workflow on hardware you already own. So this page leads with the part that actually differs: the bill, dated and sourced, with what each side does better underneath.

Not (yet) a team-wide collaboration platform — the indie filmmaker's cut of this category: the raw performance of those tools, none of the cost, your own hardware to tinker with.

the map · a three-way trade

Why two lanes exist at all

Storage for small video teams currently forces a three-way trade. Two categories claim to solve it; JuiceMount sits deliberately between them. The lanes below map both — with dated prices, and with what each one does better.

lane 01

Block-streaming SaaS

LucidLink, Suite, Shade, Aspect. The mounted-drive workflow — and a per-seat, per-TB bill that scales with exactly the thing video generates most of. Your library lives inside their filesystem.

lane 02

Self-hosted sync

Nextcloud, Seafile, Mountain Duck. Ownership — but they sync whole files. Opening a 100 GB clip to check one shot means moving 100 GB.

the third corner

Plain NFS/SMB to a NAS

Speed and ownership — and nothing else. No offline files, no cache, no WAN story, and Finder grinds on a 100 K-file library.

JuiceMount is the missing combination: partial-file streaming and Dropbox-style cache/offline semantics, at near-line-speed on your own LAN, against storage you own. There is no penalty for choosing the hybrid. That's the whole product.

lane 01 · the storage saas suites

The storage SaaS suites

LucidLink, Suite, Shade, Aspect — plus Iconik, the adjacent media asset manager (MAM) that runs on storage you already have. These products proved the mounted-drive workflow. The bill and the lock-in are the product. JuiceMount is the same workflow on hardware you already own.

The pricing, in detail — checked June 2026

LucidLink
Starter $7/user/mo (100 GB per user, plans up to 1 TB); Business $27/user/mo billed annually with 400 GB/user included, extra storage at $8 per 100 GB per month — which is $80/TB/mo for overage — and "best up to 10 TB" in their words; Enterprise is custom. lucidlink.com/pricing, checked June 2026.
Suite
Managed $75/TB/mo with 5 users included, then +$10/user/mo. BYO storage is $40/TB/mo — the mount layer alone, on a bucket you already own; on 10 TB that's $400/mo for software. suitestudios.io/pricing, checked June 2026.
Shade
Growth $29.75/seat/mo (a sale price, from $35) with 500 GB of active storage per seat, up to 15 seats; Enterprise is custom at 1 TB/seat. Their self-reported average contract: $10–15 K/yr for 10 users and 25 TB. shade.inc/pricing, checked June 2026.
Aspect
No public pricing — a free tier plus enterprise contact. Their pitch is replacing "Dropbox, LucidLink, and Frame.io in one place." aspect.inc, checked June 2026.
Iconik
Per-user tiers: Collaborator $0, Browse $9, Standard $65, Power $120 per user/mo, plus pay-per-use credits; Pro/Enterprise custom. Their positioning — "media management on storage you already have" — makes it the one product in this lane you could run on top of JuiceMount. iconik.io/pricing, checked June 2026.

What they do better

  • A managed service with someone to call. All five are businesses with support behind them. JuiceMount is self-hosted and zero-ops is not on offer: a failed disk on your NAS is your failed disk. That's the deal that makes it free.
  • Zero server setup. Sign up, install, mount — no Docker stack, no LAN to reason about. JuiceMount asks you to run a server first (about 10 minutes on TrueNAS, but it's your 10 minutes and your server from then on).
  • Collaboration and review layers. Suite's streaming engine now ships inside Frame.io Drive (announced 2026-04-17); Aspect pitches Dropbox, LucidLink, and Frame.io replaced in one place. JuiceMount has no review tools and no plans to grow them — that lane pairs fine with this.
  • Compliance certifications. Shade carries SOC 2, ISO 27001, and HIPAA attestations (their pricing page, June 2026). JuiceMount has none to show you.
  • Exhaustive metadata and AI search. Shade's neural search covers faces, transcripts, and scene descriptions; Aspect's AI agent organizes and labels footage; it is Iconik's entire product. JuiceMount does instant filename search today — content-aware search is acknowledged roadmap, not a current feature.
  • Windows and Linux clients. LucidLink in particular is mature and cross-platform with zero-knowledge encryption. JuiceMount is macOS-only today; only the server side runs anywhere.

These products are good at things JuiceMount doesn't do: managed convenience, review/approval tools, AI semantic search, Windows clients, someone to call. If you want those and the bill works for you, use them.

lane 02 · self-hosted sync

Self-hosted sync

Nextcloud, Seafile, Mountain Duck. Right instinct — own your storage. Wrong primitive for video: these tools sync files; editors need to stream blocks.

Open a 100 GB clip to check one shot and a sync tool moves 100 GB; JuiceMount streams only the blocks your NLE touches. The gap shows up on the wire too: in the author's testing these tools plateau around 0.8–1 Gbit/s even on a 10 GbE LAN — the same hardware where JuiceMount sustains roughly 7 Gbit/s in both directions. They're bound by single-stream sync engines, not the network. (Author-measured, methodology on the performance page.)

Offline semantics differ the same way. A sync tool's offline story is the full local copy — which works when the library fits the laptop, and a video library doesn't. JuiceMount pins what you choose and is honest about the rest: pinned files keep reading at SSD speed, and in offline mode un-pinned reads refuse in 4–67 ms instead of hanging Finder on a 30 s NFS retry.

And plain SMB or NFS straight to the NAS?

The third corner of the trade: speed and ownership, and nothing else. No offline files, no cache, no WAN story, and Finder grinds on a 100 K-file library. JuiceMount keeps the LAN speed and adds the missing parts — directory listings answer from local SQLite in 15–120 ms on a 100 K+-entry volume, pinned projects survive the network, and the same volume mounts over a WAN (hotel Wi-Fi with pinned files and the write spool is a supported shape).

What they do better

  • Maturity and community. Nextcloud and Seafile are mature platforms with huge communities and years of production deployments. JuiceMount is pre-1.0 and says so.
  • Clients everywhere. Windows, Linux, Android, iOS — and the mobile clients are genuinely good. JuiceMount is macOS-only today.
  • Simple shares. A share link you can hand to anyone is built in. JuiceMount has nothing like it.
  • Documents and general sync. Whole-file sync is the right primitive for a contracts folder. Many shops should run Nextcloud for docs next to JuiceMount for media — use both.
  • Mountain Duck is a fine generalist. $49 one-time to mount almost any bucket or server for everyday files. It isn't built for scrubbing OCF over the wire, and doesn't claim to be.

the honest outs

Pick the other thing if…

Consistent with the README's "What JuiceMount is not" — stating this up front saves everyone time.

  • Your library is small and the work is collaboration-heavy. A SaaS suite's review tools, managed storage, and someone-to-call earn their bill at that shape. If the bill works for you, use them.
  • You need Windows or Linux editors today. JuiceMount is macOS-only (the server side runs anywhere Docker does). LucidLink or the sync tools cover mixed shops now.
  • You want content-aware AI search now. Shade, Aspect, or Iconik — for JuiceMount it's acknowledged roadmap, and Iconik in particular runs on storage you already have.
  • Nobody wants to run a server. JuiceMount is not zero-ops: a failed disk on your NAS is your failed disk. If that trade reads wrong, managed SaaS is the right call.
  • You need browser review and approvals. Frame.io and friends own that lane — and pair fine with this.
  • It's documents, not media. Nextcloud and Seafile are the right primitive for general files. Plenty of shops will run both.
