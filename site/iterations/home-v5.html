    demo 04 · blocks, not files

    Your files become blocks. That's the whole trick.

    Most tools treat a video file as one object — to touch any of it, you move all of it. JuiceMount stores every file on your NAS as 4 MB blocks (JuiceFS's open, documented format), and blocks travel independently. First the idea; then watch it happen to a real file.

    the plain NAS share — SMB
    MacNAS

    Every read pays the wire

    A mapped SMB share keeps no real cache on your Mac, so every open, seek, and scrub is a fresh round trip to the NAS. On the LAN that's constant friction; over a VPN it's an afternoon lost — and Finder search has to walk the whole tree.

    the sync class
    Maccloud

    Whole-file tools

    Dropbox, Nextcloud, Seafile, Mountain Duck: the unit of transfer is the file. A clip can't play until the whole file lands, so checking one shot in a 100 GB clip means moving 100 GB — and one changed frame re-syncs the file, not the frame.

    block storage — JuiceMount
    MacNAS

    Blocks, plus a cache that stays

    Files live on your NAS as 4 MB blocks: the mount opens a file without fetching it, streams only the blocks you touch, and keeps them in an SSD cache that survives — your working set stops paying the wire at all. And the format is not a moat: your bytes sit in JuiceFS's open, documented chunk format; the stock juicefs client mounts the volume with no JuiceMount involved.

    Honest math, simplified picture: the 24 squares stand in for the real count — an 8 GB file in 4 MB chunks is 2,000 chunks, and three chunks read back is 12 MB. How many chunks a three-second read touches depends on codec and bitrate; what doesn't change is the unit of transfer. Sync-class behavior is described at the category level, checked June 2026 — individual clients vary and evolve.

    Speed, resilience, and ownership — without picking two

    A plain SMB share round-trips every read. Self-hosted sync owns the bytes but moves whole files. Storage SaaS streams blocks but bills forever. JuiceMount is the missing combination.

    speed 7 Gbit/s

    Fast because it's your LAN

    Roughly 7 Gbit/s in both directions on a 10 GbE LAN, author-measured — the same wire where sync-class tools capped near 1 Gbit/s in the same tests. Cached reads come off local SSD at 226–571 MB/s; a 100 K-file library opens folders in 15–120 ms.

    resilience 4–67 ms

    Built for the day the network is gone

    Pin a project and keep cutting on a plane. In offline mode, un-pinned reads refuse in 4–67 ms instead of hanging Finder on a 30 s NFS retry. The opt-in write spool acks renders on local SSD and trickle-uploads in the background, SHA-256-verified at every hop.

    economics $0

    Software is free; the hardware is yours

    JuiceMount is open source, Apache-2.0. Suite charges $75/TB/mo managed — or $40/TB/mo to mount a bucket you already own (checked June 2026). A one-time box beats a forever bill sooner than you'd think.

    See when the hardware pays for itself

    All of it runs on the NAS you already have — TrueNAS SCALE is the production-tested path (paste-the-YAML install), and anything that runs Docker works: Synology DSM 7+, QNAP, Unraid, or a plain Linux box. The setup guide walks every step.

    Performance figures are the author's measurements on his own Apple-Silicon Mac ↔ TrueNAS rig, not independent benchmarks — methodology, workload scripts, and the regression harness are public.

    The technology stack

    For the technically curious. Your NLE talks to a normal Finder volume — an NFS share served from localhost, tuned for Finder. Behind it the hot path stays on your Mac: SQLite for metadata, an SSD block cache with pinning, a write spool that keeps uploads moving on slow connections. Underneath, JuiceFS breaks file data into chunks stored as S3 objects on your NAS, with metadata in Redis.

    Architecture notes, including the security model, live in the repo

    What it's not (yet)

    not (yet)

    JuiceMount is not a team-wide, multi-department collaboration platform. No review pages or approvals, no AI media search (filename search is instant today; content-aware search is roadmap), no Windows client — macOS only, and you run the NAS. One caveat from the README worth repeating: identical paths on every Mac is the designed multi-machine workflow, but heavy simultaneous multi-editor use hasn't been soak-tested yet.

    what it is

    The indie filmmaker and small-post-team tool: the raw performance of the Suite / Shade / Iconik / LucidLink category, none of the cost, on hardware you can tinker with. A real mount, a status system that tells the truth, and chunks you can walk away with.

    what's coming

    The near roadmap, in the order it's scored: codec-aware Quick Look (spacebar previews for R3D, ARRI, BRAW, ProRes RAW), traffic-light backup verification on every project, bandwidth-aware auto-switching to offline mode on cellular, and project snapshots — the full roadmap is public. On the drawing board beyond it: a larger, Finder-adjacent app for browsing the library with richer metadata.

    The full what-it's-not list lives in the README

    Your bytes are your bytes.

    JuiceMount mounts your own NAS as a Finder volume that behaves like LucidLink and costs like the hardware: scrub 100 GB files over the network touching only the blocks you play, pin projects for offline, write at local-SSD speed while uploads trickle in the background. No per-seat fee. No per-TB fee. No storage contract.

    And leaving stays free in both directions: the built-in migration tool moves an existing library in, and your bytes sit in JuiceFS's open, documented format the whole time — the stock juicefs client reads the volume with no JuiceMount involved. The exit story, animated.

    S3 and cloud collaboration have their place — this changes the cost equation for small teams so speed doesn't force inferior infrastructure.

    What you'll need: a Mac on macOS 14+ and any box that runs Docker — a TrueNAS, a Synology, an old tower. The server stack is one docker compose up; the README's quick-start walks the rest.
