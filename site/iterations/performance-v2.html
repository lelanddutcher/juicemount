the numbers, with receipts

7 Gbit/s to hardware you own.

On a 10 GbE LAN, JuiceMount sustains roughly 7 Gbit/s in both directions — author-measured, not vendor-benchmarked. This page is every performance number we publish, how it was produced, a plain list of what it doesn't prove — and two demos you can drive to watch the blocks move.

  • ~7 Gbit/s sustained throughput, 10 GbE LAN, up and down (author-measured)
  • 226–571 MB/s cached reads through the full NFS path
  • 15–120 ms directory open on a 100 K+-entry volume
  • ~29 ms filename search across ~131 K entries
  • 4–67 ms un-pinned read refusal in offline mode

Every number on this page was measured by the author on his own setup — Apple-Silicon Mac ↔ TrueNAS SCALE over 10 GbE. Honest measurements, not marketing benchmarks; your hardware will differ. Methodology, workload scripts, and the regression harness are public.

measured 01 · throughput

Throughput, in context

For comparison on the same 10 GbE link, Mountain Duck/Seafile-class mounted-bucket tools measured ~0.8–1 Gbit/s in the author's testing — they're bound by single-stream sync engines, not the network.

Category behavior as of June 2026 — check current client docs, these tools evolve. At 10 GbE the remaining ceiling is honest physics: disks, single-link line rate, and protocol overhead.

measured 02 · latency

Latency, where editors feel it

Metadata is the thing Finder hammers hardest, so it never leaves local SQLite on the hot path: directory opens that take 3–10 s through raw FUSE complete in 15–120 ms. Filename search across ~131 K entries answers in ~29 ms, and in offline mode an un-pinned read refuses in 4–67 ms instead of a 30 s NFS retry hang.

measured 03 · the cache

What the cache does to the wire

Cached reads run 226–571 MB/s through the full NFS path, READ p95 481 µs. The fully-cached 200 MiB sequential read did 431 MB/s with 4.6 MB total network traffic — and with the network off entirely, a pinned 350 MB file read at 215+ MB/s sustained. This is the measurement the warm lane above replays.

methodology · public harness

How the numbers were produced

The rig: the author's Apple-Silicon Mac ↔ TrueNAS SCALE over 10 GbE. The instrument: the repo's own performance harness — the same battery that gates releases.

  • Workloads model real NLE and Finder behavior, not synthetic IO: resolve-scrub sustains the ~60–240 random READs/sec a DaVinci scrub generates; finder-copy-deep runs Finder copy cascades with AppleDouble sidecars; bin-browse walks directory trees the way a bin browser does; cold-playback reads fully-uncached files sequentially; pin-coverage-verify re-prefetches an entire pin root.
  • Each run snapshots the control plane's /metrics before and after a fixed wall-clock window (typically 30–60 s) and emits per-RPC latency percentiles — p50, p95, p99, max — plus throughput deltas. That's where figures like READ p95 481 µs come from.
  • Baselines are stored per build SHA, and a regression orchestrator fails any build where an RPC type's p95 doubles or throughput halves against baseline. Workloads are re-run under chaos — Redis flaps, throttled MinIO, forced metadata syncs — to surface the next latent bug class, not to flatter the numbers.

The full contract — workload scripts, thresholds, baseline lifecycle — is published in docs/PERFORMANCE_METHODOLOGY.md; the scripts live in the repo under scripts/qa-suite/.

scope

What we don't claim

This is not a benchmark of named competitors' current builds. The sync-tool ceiling above is what Mountain Duck/Seafile-class tools measured in the author's testing, on his rig, at the time — category behavior as of June 2026; check current client docs, these tools evolve.

These are point-in-time author measurements on one Mac and one server, not independent benchmarks. No vendor lab, no tuned showcase — and no claim beyond what the harness reproduces. The two demos on this page are illustrative models built on these measurements plus link line-rate math, and they say so where they say anything.

Your rig will differ — run it yourself.

The harness compares any two builds on your hardware, and a report from a different NAS, switch, or NLE is a genuinely useful contribution, whether it beats these numbers or not.
